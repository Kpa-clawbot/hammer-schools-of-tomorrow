üîé Parent Analysis & Insights

üí° The committee explicitly said core class size is the #1 priority. Option 2 leads this metric by a wide margin (74% vs 55% vs 44%). This alone makes Option 2 the frontrunner.

Why Option 2 Wins

  • Committee called Option 2 "most favorable" for class size ‚Äî their stated top priority
  • Committee called Option 1 "least favorable" for class size
  • Option 2 also leads on equity (bilingual program placement)
  • Lowest per-student cost: $647 (saves the most money)
  • Fewest students separated from classmates: 410 (vs 639-647)

Walkability ‚Äî The Counterargument Debunked

‚ö†Ô∏è Option 1 supporters may argue walkability. But the difference between Options 1 and 2 is negligible: about 200 feet / 30‚Äì60 seconds of walking. Meanwhile, the 90th percentile travel distance is actually better in Option 2 (1.56 mi vs 1.92 mi for Option 1) ‚Äî meaning the longest commutes are shorter.

Why Hammer Goes to Carson in Option 1

In Option 1, Rachel Carson closes as a neighborhood school. Hammer Montessori would relocate there to provide Carson-area families a walkable magnet school option as a form of mitigation. This is not about Hammer's benefit ‚Äî it's about softening the blow for Carson families.

Option 3 Considerations

Option 3 is less favorable overall but does prioritize IEP equity ‚Äî Special Day Classes stay at 72% ideal (tied with status quo). However, it closes Galarza (Hammer's shared campus), potentially disrupting the campus dynamic even though Hammer itself stays.

üö® Option 3 Vulnerability Warning: Under Option 3, Hammer alone would occupy a campus designed for 758 students ‚Äî with only 313 enrolled. That's 41.3% utilization. A half-empty campus is a prime target for future consolidation rounds. Option 3 doesn't just hurt Galarza ‚Äî it puts Hammer's long-term viability at serious risk.

Capacity Correction (Parent Analysis)

The campus site is designed for 758 students total. Current combined enrollment is Hammer 313 + Galarza 331 = 644, which means the campus is at 85% utilization ‚Äî healthy and efficient. Under Option 2, the campus could absorb up to 114 additional students to reach 100% capacity ‚Äî not 250 as was previously estimated by some.

Equity Dimension

An analysis of the closure proposals found that school closures across all three options disproportionately affect Latino students, with an estimated 1,300‚Äì1,700 Latino students impacted. This raises concerns under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act, which prohibits discrimination in federally-funded programs. Parents have flagged this to the committee as an additional factor favoring Option 2, which displaces the fewest students overall.
